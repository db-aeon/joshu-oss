joshu Brand Guidelines
1. Brand Overview & Mission
joshu (助手) — Japanese for "assistant" — is a personal AI assistant app designed for people who run things: builders, writers, makers, operators. It's positioned as a thinking tool; specifically, "a bicycle for the mind, built for people running things."
Tagline
"Helpful first. Charming second."
This is the north star. Joshu's primary value is utility and genuine helpfulness. Any charm must serve that mission; it never overshadows it.
Brand Essence
Joshu occupies a specific emotional space: 80s/90s computer optimism meets the texture of memory.
Imagine the Knowledge Navigator video (1987)—that vision of ambient, patient, almost understated technological helpfulness—except the assistant doesn't look like a character from a pitch deck. It looks like someone you went to high school with. Confident technology, human imperfection. The machine is brilliant. The feeling is familiar.

2. Brand Positioning & Audience
Target Audience
Joshu serves knowledge workers, creators, and operators who value clarity and directness. They are:
Intellectually curious but pragmatic
Skeptical of hype; responsive to restraint
Users of tools like Figma, Obsidian, Notion, but who dislike the design excesses those tools have accumulated
Comfortable with technology; unimpressed by unnecessary decoration
They read, they think, they make decisions
Brand Positioning Statement
Joshu is the thinking tool that gets out of your way. It's not a mascot, a pet, or a character. It's not performing intelligence. It is intelligent, and it knows that you can feel the difference.
In a market saturated with AI products that default to gloss, whimsy, and performance, joshu stands for the opposite: restraint, directness, and the confidence that a truly helpful tool doesn't need to be cute.

3. Brand Voice & Tone
Voice Characteristics
Confident: Knows what it's talking about. Never hedges or apologizes unnecessarily.
Lightly witty: Humor is allowed, but only when it serves clarity or helps the user. Never forced or meme-adjacent.
Never cloying: No baby talk, no unnecessary exclamation points, no false enthusiasm.
Knowledgeable without performance: Doesn't announce how smart it is; doesn't perform thinking. Shows up ready.
Respectful of the user's time: Short, direct, useful.
Tone Examples
Joshu says:
"Here's what I found. Let me know what questions that raises."
"This isn't in my training. I'd check with [resource]."
"This might take a minute."
Joshu does NOT say:
"Wow, what a great question! ✨" (cloying)
"Huzzah! Time to brainstorm!" (performative)
"I'm an AI, so I can't actually know, but..." (hedging)
"Let me think about that" [spinning cursor for 2 seconds] (false performance of thought)

5. The joshu Story / Narrative
The Origin
Joshu is the assistant you never quite see. It arrives like a notification, a whisper, a moment of clarity when you're stuck. It doesn't announce itself. It doesn't introduce itself. It just appears when it's needed, and it has what you're looking for.
The visual expression of this narrative is the joshu Portrait: each user's joshu appears as a faded found photograph. The kind of picture you find in a used bookstore or at an estate sale. Slightly out of focus. Slightly awkward framing. A real person—someone at a desk, in a doorway, on a porch—captured at a moment that happened to last forever.
This is not an idealized avatar. This is not a cartoon. This is the visual anchor to the idea that the best assistants are the ones who feel present but not intrusive, useful but not overwhelming.
The Touchstone: Knowledge Navigator
Apple's 1987 Knowledge Navigator video imagined a future where technology was helpful, patient, and conversational—but never cute. The assistant in that video wasn't a character. It was a presence. Joshu takes that core feeling and translates it for 2024: a tool that's helpful first, and charms as a side effect of being genuine.

6. Logo & Mark System
Primary Mark: The Pixelated Cursor
The joshu logo is a pixelated pointing finger cursor—inspired by Susan Kare's work on early Macintosh interfaces.
Grid specifications:
Base grid: 8×8 pixels (icon size)
Expanded grid: 16×16 pixels (wordmark and detailed applications)
Built on pixel-perfect foundations; no anti-aliasing unless scaling down below 32px
Visual Description:
A single right-pointing hand cursor with a distinct pointing finger. The silhouette is confident and clear. It recalls the 1984-1991 era of computer interface design but does not feel nostalgic—it feels intentional.
Wordmark Lockup
The wordmark consists of the pixelated pointing finger combined with "joshu" set in Work Sans SemiBold, lowercase, all on one horizontal line.
Clear Space Rules:
Minimum clear space: 8px on all sides (or 1× the height of the cursor mark)
The cursor and wordmark form a single unit; they cannot be separated except in specific applications (icon-only for app launcher, etc.)
The wordmark is always lowercase, never stacked
Kanji Usage:
The Japanese kanji 助手 (joshu) is used as a quiet secondary anchor in brand contexts only—never in product UI. It appears on business cards, in brand documents, or in cultural/heritage contexts. It never appears as a primary mark or on primary user-facing product surfaces.

Clear Space & Isolation
When the cursor appears without wordmark (e.g., app icon, favicon), it must sit centered within a square clear space equal to at least 1× its own width on all sides.
Misuse & What NOT to Do
❌ Do NOT add macron to the o: "jōshu" is wrong in all contexts. Always "joshu."
❌ Do NOT rotate, skew, or digitally distort the cursor. Pixel-perfect alignment is non-negotiable.
❌ Do NOT stack the wordmark vertically. The cursor and text are always horizontal.
❌ Do NOT add drop shadows, glows, or effects to the mark itself (glow states are reserved for interactive states in UI only).
❌ Do NOT use the kanji 助手 on primary user interfaces or app marketing. It is for heritage/cultural contexts only.
❌ Do NOT scale below 16px without design review; below 8px is not legible.

7. Color System
The Faded Photograph Palette
Joshu's color system is inspired by sun-faded photographs and the warm, slightly muted feeling of 1980s and early 1990s print materials. The palette is restrained. Most of the interface is off-white and neutral. Color is used sparingly, with intention.
Color Philosophy
The one thing that's completely awake: In a warm, faded, memory-like palette, ACTION blue (#0057FF) is the only color that feels fully present and alert. It is reserved exclusively for moments of interaction: hover states, active buttons, focused form fields, the cursor glow on hover.
Using ACTION blue anywhere else dilutes its signal. It says "I work, and I'm ready." When users see that blue, they know they can do something.
Paper and Neutrals: PAPER is the dominant surface. It's warm but not saturated—like a slightly aged book page. ROSE and SAGE are used for quiet visual hierarchy and support. They never dominate.
Accents are Sparing: PEACH and YELLOW are used for moments that need warmth or attention, but sparingly. A search highlight, a positive message, a gentle call-out. Not for everything.
Accessibility & Contrast
All body text (regular weight) meets WCAG AA contrast minimums against PAPER (4.5:1).
ACTION blue text on PAPER meets WCAG AAA standards (7:1).
When using SAGE for secondary text, minimum size is 14px to maintain legibility.
Color in Context
Backgrounds: PAPER (dominant), SKY (soft containers)
Text: Body text in black or near-black; secondary text in SAGE
Accents: ROSE for borders/dividers; PEACH for warm highlights; YELLOW for search/markers
Interactive: ACTION blue ONLY for buttons, links, cursor states, focus indicators
Disabled/Inactive: SAGE text or 40% opacity over primary color

8. Typography System
Primary Typeface: Work Sans
Work Sans (Google Fonts, open source) is joshu's primary typeface across all applications.
Weights in use:
Light (300) — not recommended for body text, use for large display or very low-contrast contexts only
Regular (400) — body text, UI labels, default
Medium (500) — slightly emphasized text, form labels, secondary headings
SemiBold (600) — wordmark, primary headings, navigation
Bold (700) — emphasis, important headings, CTAs
Specifications:
Secondary Type: Pixel / Terminal Accent
Restrained pixel type is used sparingly as an accent only. It appears in:
System labels (e.g., "LOADING", "ERROR", "COMPLETE")
Terminal or code-like contexts
Pixel-native UI moments (HyperCard-style window chrome)
Pixel type is never used decoratively for its own sake and never dominates a page or interface.
Type Hierarchy Principles
Restraint over decoration: No flourishes, no serifs, no display fonts used frivolously.
Generous line spacing: Breathing room helps joshu feel unhurried and clear.
Deliberate weight contrast: Headings use SemiBold; body uses Regular. This creates hierarchy without clutter.
Lowercase for personality: joshu's wordmark and many labels are lowercase. This is intentional—it conveys approachability without cuteness.

9. Imagery & Photography Direction
The joshu Portrait: Signature Visual Move
Each user's joshu is represented by a faded found photograph—a real person captured in a specific moment, rendered as if discovered in a used bookstore or estate sale. This is joshu's most distinctive visual asset and the core of its humanity.
Photography Style Specifications
Era: 1987–1992 drug-store print quality
Light leaks are acceptable and encouraged
Color cast is expected; slightly warm or cool tones add character
Slight grain and texture from printing and aging
Uneven framing suggests the photo wasn't taken by a professional
Soft focus elements add to the "memory" feeling
Subjects:
Real people in their natural environment
At a desk, in a doorway, on a porch, in an office, at home
Not idealized or posed stiffly; slightly awkward is better
Specific and particular, never generic or stock
Mid-shot preferred (shoulders or waist up)
What NOT to do:
❌ Do NOT use idealized stock photography or AI-generated faces
❌ Do NOT airbrushed or overly retouched images
❌ Do NOT character illustrations or mascots
❌ Do NOT contemporary professional photography (too sharp, too clean)
❌ Do NOT composite multiple images or add digital effects
Technical Specifications
Aspect Ratio: 1:1 (square) or 4:3 (portrait-oriented)
Dimensions:
Icon size: 96×96px (square)
Card/detail: 400×400px or 300×400px
Large context (onboarding, profile): up to 600×800px
Treatment Process:
Source real photographs from 1987–1992 era (thrift stores, archives, friends' parents' collections)
Scan at 300 DPI minimum
Apply light color correction only—warm white balance, slightly muted saturation
Add subtle film grain overlay (film stock suggestion: Kodak Gold 200 or Fujifilm Superia)
No sharpening; slight blur/softness is desired
Slight vignette (10–15% darkening at edges) helps frame the subject
Export as high-quality JPEG or PNG with maintained grain texture
Usage in Product
Joshu portraits appear in:
User profile / avatar (primary)
Onboarding flow
Settings / account page
In-app identity moments
Marketing materials (sparingly, with permission)
Each portrait is paired with a person's name and role or context. The photo is not a mascot—it's a representation of the human who's helping, the presence behind the tool.

10. Iconography & Graphic Language
Pixel-Native Icon System
Joshu uses a sparse, carefully curated icon system inspired by Susan Kare's work on early Macintosh interfaces. Icons are:
Grid-based: 8px and 16px grids; pixel-perfect alignment
Minimal: Remove all non-essential details; clarity is paramount
Confident: No fuzzy edges, no anti-aliasing below 32px
Functional: Each icon represents a specific action or concept; no decorative icons
Consistent: Stroke weight, proportions, and visual weight are consistent across the set
Icon Categories
Icon Style Rules
Stroke weight: 1px minimum (1px on 16×16; 2px on 32×32 and above)
No fills except for primary actions (ACTION blue background)
Line-based, not solid fills, except where indicated
Rounded corners are avoided; perpendicular strokes only
Proportions are square unless the icon's meaning demands otherwise
HyperCard-Style Frames & Dividers
Joshu borrows visual language from HyperCard and early Mac windowing systems:
Window frames: Thin lines (#C8A2A6 ROSE or #A3839A SAGE) with ample padding
Dividers: Subtle horizontal or vertical lines separating sections
Inset buttons: Buttons can have a subtle inset/beveled appearance (reminiscent of physical button press)
Content cards: Bordered containers with generous padding and off-white or SKY backgrounds
These elements are used to create visual separation and hierarchy without heavy shadows or glassmorphism effects.
Graphic Patterns & Textures
Paper Grain: A subtle film grain texture is applied as an overlay across most UI surfaces, particularly backgrounds. This adds warmth and texture without being distracting. Opacity: 2–5%.
Grid Background (optional, in specific contexts): A very faint pixel grid (1px lines, 8px spacing) can appear in code editors, terminal-like contexts, or specialized input areas. Opacity: 1–2%, color: SAGE.

11. UI & Product Design Principles
Design Philosophy
Joshu's interface is built on a few core principles:
Generous cream space: White space is not wasted space. It's breathing room. Use it.
Gridded, confident layouts: Align to an 8px grid. Predictability is comforting.
No rounded-everything: Avoid the trend of rounding all corners. Use sharp corners for strength; reserved rounded corners (4–8px) for soft moments.
No glassmorphism, no shadows-on-shadows: These effects obscure clarity. Joshu is direct.
Original windowing GUI language: Borrow from HyperCard, early Mac OS, and BeOS. Not macOS Big Sur. This is intentional.
Interaction is ACTION blue: The only place users see that bright blue is when they can do something. It's a signal.
Specific UI Patterns
Buttons
Primary CTA: Solid ACTION blue (#0057FF) background, white text, Medium weight, 14px
Secondary: ROSE border, black text, transparent background
Tertiary: Text only (black), subtle underline on hover
Disabled: 40% opacity
Hover state: Primary buttons: slight brightness increase, small scale up (102%), glow effect
Padding: 10px vertical, 16px horizontal (minimum)
Corners: Slightly rounded (4px) or sharp; be consistent within context
Forms & Inputs
Input fields: PAPER background with ROSE or SAGE border (1px)
Focus state: ACTION blue glow (4px, opacity 30%), no change to border color
Label: Work Sans Medium, 14px, placed above input (left-aligned)
Helper text: Work Sans Regular, 12px, SAGE color, below input
Error state: Red/warm error color (#D94E4E or similar), with icon and message
Placeholder text: SAGE color, italic optional (not required)
Cards & Containers
Borders: ROSE or subtle SAGE dividers (1px)
Background: SKY or PAPER
Padding: 16px minimum
Shadows: None; use borders for separation
Corner radius: 0px (sharp) or 4px (reserved for softer moments)
Search & Results
Search bar: PAPER background, SAGE border, ACTION blue focus glow
Result highlights: YELLOW background (very light, opacity 20%) or YELLOW text
Result cards: Bordered containers, left-aligned, generous padding
Result metadata: SAGE color, smaller type
Messages & Feedback
Success: Soft green (#4CAF50) or PEACH background, with icon
Error: Warm red (#D94E4E) or alert tone, with icon
Warning: YELLOW or orange tone, with icon
Info: ACTION blue or SKY background, with icon
Busy/Loading: Hourglass cursor, subtle animation

13. Do's & Don'ts
DO
✅ DO use ACTION blue for interaction only. Buttons, links, hover states, cursor glows, focus indicators.
✅ DO maintain pixel-perfect alignment. The cursor and icons are grid-based. Precision is part of the brand.
✅ DO use generous white space. Cream space, breathing room, and empty pixels are assets.
✅ DO write in joshu's voice: direct, confident, lightly witty, never cloying.
✅ DO use the joshu Portrait for user representation. Real, specific, faded photographs—not avatars or illustrations.
✅ DO borrow from HyperCard and early Mac windowing language for UI structure and chrome.
✅ DO use Work Sans consistently for all typography across product and marketing.
✅ DO source imagery from real 1987–1992 drug-store prints. Scan them, add gentle grain and color correction, use them as portraits.
✅ DO apply subtle paper grain texture to backgrounds to add warmth and tactile quality.
✅ DO use ROSE and SAGE for secondary UI elements. They're quiet but present.
✅ DO maintain the one-line horizontal lockup for the wordmark. Cursor + "joshu" (lowercase) together, never stacked.
✅ DO test contrast and accessibility with WCAG standards in mind.
✅ DO document pixel-perfect specs for icons and interface elements. Consistency is quality.
DON'T
❌ DON'T use macron on joshu. Never "jōshu." Always "joshu."
❌ DON'T stack the wordmark or separate the cursor from the text. They're a unit.
❌ DON'T add effects to the cursor mark (shadows, glows, distortions). Glow is reserved for interactive states in UI only.
❌ DON'T use bright pastels without grounding them. The palette needs ACTION blue and grain to avoid feeling saccharine.
❌ DON'T make joshu cute, ever. No blobs, no smiley faces, no mascots, no baby talk, no character voices.
❌ DON'T use vaporwave irony or Y2K chrome. Retro should feel intentional, not ironic.
❌ DON'T apply glassmorphism, heavy shadows, or blur effects. They obscure clarity.
❌ DON'T round all corners. Use sharp corners for strength; reserve rounded corners (4–8px) for specific moments.
❌ DON'T use corporate stock photography. Source real photographs; shoot new ones if needed.
❌ DON'T make design decisions based on "because it looks cool." Everything should serve clarity and helpfulness first.
❌ DON'T use the kanji 助手 in product interfaces or primary marketing. It's for heritage contexts only.
❌ DON'T mix typefaces casually. Work Sans is primary. Pixel type is accent only.
❌ DON'T scale UI elements below their grid minimum. 8×8px is the floor for icons; 16px is preferred.
❌ DON'T use ACTION blue for anything other than interaction. Dilutes the signal. Blue means "you can do something."
❌ DON'T perform emotion in copy or design. Let clarity and utility do the work. Users will feel the difference.
❌ DON'T forget that joshu's primary value is helpfulness. Charm is secondary. Always choose clarity over cuteness.
